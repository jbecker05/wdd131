Objection Playbook

This page gives you ready-to-use responses for the most common objections you’ll hear on the sales floor. Use these as a starting point, then tweak the wording so it still sounds like you.

How to use this playbook

  1. Find the objection that matches what your customer says.
  2. Read the sample response out loud until it feels natural.
  3. Add your own examples, questions, or stories that fit your product.
  4. Practice the objection on the flashcard trainer.

The goal isn’t to sound scripted. The goal is to be prepared so you can stay calm, confident, and helpful when a customer hesitates.

Money & price objections

“I need to talk to my wife first.”

Goal:

Understand what matters to the other decision-maker and keep the momentum.

Sample response:

You: Totally understand. A lot of my customers like to talk it over with their spouse first.

So we make the most of your time here, what questions do you think your wife will ask? That way you can walk out with all the answers ready.

Follow-up questions:

  • “What matters most to her in a purchase like this?”
  • “Is she more focused on price, protection, or convenience?”

“It’s too expensive.”

Goal:

Reframe the price around value and long-term savings.

Sample response:

You: I hear you. Nobody wants to overpay. If we look past the upfront price for a second, this actually saves you money if something goes wrong.

For example, if your screen cracked just once, the repair alone would cost more than this whole protection plan. Would it be helpful if I walked through a real-world scenario?

Follow-up questions:

  • “What would be the most frustrating thing to pay for out of pocket?”
  • “Have you ever had to pay for a repair like that before?”

“Let me think about it.”

Goal:

Uncover the real concern behind the delay.

Sample response:

You: For sure, it’s an important decision. When people tell me they want to think about it, it’s usually because they’re unsure about one or two things.

If you had to pick, what’s the biggest question still in your mind so I can clear that up before you go?

Follow-up questions:

  • “Is it more about the price, the coverage, or the timing?”
  • “What would make this a ‘no-brainer’ for you?”

Trust & timing objections

“I don’t really need that.”

Goal:

Respect their point of view while educating them on risk.

Sample response:

You: You might be right, and I never want to push anything you don’t need.

Most people who choose this didn’t think they’d need it either. They just wanted to be covered if something happened. Would it be okay if I showed you the top two things this protects you from?

Follow-up questions:

  • “If something did happen, how big of a headache would that be?”
  • “Would you rather pay a little now or risk paying a lot later?”

“Can I come back later?”

Goal:

Keep the door open and make it easy to commit now.

Sample response:

You: You’re always welcome to come back. The only reason people usually handle it now is because they’re already here, and it saves them an extra trip.

If we could make this quick and simple, would there be any other reason not to get it taken care of today?

Follow-up questions:

  • “Is it more about time or budget right now?”
  • “What would make it easier to move forward today?”

Customize these for your style

These scripts are meant to sound like a real conversation, not a robot. Once you find a few lines you like, rewrite them in your own words and practice them on the flashcards until they feel natural.

Tip: Record yourself saying the script, then listen back. If it sounds awkward, tweak the wording until it matches how you really talk to customers.
